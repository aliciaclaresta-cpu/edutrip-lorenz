Ritsumeikan Asian Pacific University (APU)

The moment we arrived at the university we could already see a lot of international students walking around the campus. English is used a lot, so it’s easy to imagine studying there. The campus itself is located on a hillside, offering impressive views, though accessibility can be slightly challenging due to the steep location. Academically, APU seems to emphasize global perspectives and cross-cultural communication. Overall, it gives the impression of a university that prioritizes diversity, openness, and international collaboration.


Kyushu University

Kyushu University presents a more academic and research-focused environment. The campus is very large, modern, and well-equipped, especially in terms of facilities for science and technology. The social atmosphere seemed quieter, so this may reflect a stronger emphasis on academic rigor. Kyushu University appears to be particularly suitable for students interested in STEM fields and research-oriented learning.


KEIO University (APU)

Keio University gives a clear impression of prestige and tradition. As one of Japan’s well-known private universities, the campus shows a strong academic reputation and a competitive environment. Students at Keio appear driven and goal-oriented, especially in fields like business and economics. The university seems to offer strong networking opportunities and industry connections. Overall, Keio is well-suited for students aiming for high academic achievement and future career opportunities.


Tokyo University

Tokyo university is considered as one of the top universities in Japan, known for its strong academic reputation, competitive admissions, and influential alumni. Just by visiting the campus, the strong sense of history,discipline, and excellence, and prestige can be felt immediately. One unique and interesting student culture is how many undergraduates like to take pictures in front of iconic campus landmarks, especially the famous Yasuda Auditorium. These photos are not just for memories, but often seen as a form of personal motivation or “manifestation.” Students take these pictures as a way to visualize themselves studying there in the future, reinforcing their goals and ambitions.
