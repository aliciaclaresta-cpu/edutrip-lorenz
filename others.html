Tokyo Waste Management Center

The Tokyo Waste Management Center is where 23 wards in Japan process their waste in a unique way. Instead of piling up trash until it stinks, the center was built on reclaimed land and incinerated by category until it turned to ash. Fifty years ago, this area was a body of water. Then, sturdy walls were built from the seabed to the surface. The water was then sucked out of the walls until dry and sand was added. This process took approximately 10 years.


Tokyo’s landfill system is designed to minimize environmental impact. Rainwater filtering through waste becomes dark leachate, while decomposition produces methane gas, which previously caused fires. These risks are now reduced using the “sandwich method,” where layers of waste and soil are alternated up to about 30 meters to compress and stabilize the landfill.


Leachate is collected, partially treated, and reused for dust control, while the remainder is further purified before being safely discharged into Tokyo Bay. The site is also used for educational visits, and efficient management is crucial as landfill space is expected to last only about 50 years.



Disaster Prevention Center

The visit to Sona Area Tokyo gave us a practical learning experience. We were given a chance to experience a disaster simulation where we had to evacuate through a city recreated after an earthquake. During the activity, we used a tablet to answer quiz questions, which made the learning more interactive and engaging. It helped us understand what to do in real emergency situations, not just memorize information. What makes this place important is that it is also designed as a real disaster prevention base, meaning it can actually be used during major disasters. This makes the experience feel more realistic and meaningful.



Homestay

The homestay was one of the most awaited parts of the education trip, as it gave us a chance to experience local life in Tarumizu. Tarumizu is a coastal city located in Kagoshima Prefecture on the Osumi Peninsula in southern Kyushu, Japan. Situated on the east side of Kinko Bay, it lies directly across from Kagoshima City and the active Sakurajima volcano, that you can see in the video, making it a scenic area known for fishing and nature.



During the Homestay program, we could experience wearing a kimono, making dangos, interacting with the locals, cultural exchange, and many more. The house itself had a semi modern-traditional design, with a calm and comfortable atmosphere that reflected Japanese culture. On the second day, we were lucky to experience snow, which made the stay even more special. In addition, we learned how to make various Japanese snacks, which was both fun and educational. It allowed us to interact more with our host families and understand their lifestyle better. Overall, the homestay was a meaningful experience that helped us appreciate Japanese culture, daily life, and hospitality.



The meals prepared by our host families were especially memorable. The food felt very warm and homemade, and it showed the care and effort they put into welcoming us. It was very different from restaurant food and made the experience more personal. We also experienced using a furo (Japanese bath), which was something new since it is not common in Indonesia. It was a unique cultural experience and helped us understand daily life in Japan more deeply.



Toyota Industrial Factory

Visiting Toyota gave us insight into how a global company operates with high efficiency and precision. We learned about the Toyota Production System, which focuses on continuous improvement and minimizing waste. From a student perspective, it was interesting to see how automation and human work are combined in the production line. Everything is organized and systematic, showing how discipline and innovation are important in large-scale industries. This visit helped us understand real-world applications of technology and management.



Miraikan Science Museum

At Miraikan, we explored various interactive science and technology exhibits. The museum focuses on future innovations such as robotics, space, and environmental issues. One of the highlights was seeing advanced robots and interactive displays that made complex concepts easier to understand.


Unlike traditional museums, Miraikan allows students to learn by experiencing, which makes the visit more engaging. It shows how science is continuously developing and how it can impact the future. It encouraged us to think more about innovation, technology, and global challenges.
